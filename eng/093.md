Setting It Up, Knocking It Down

Most of the time, you’ll use Reactions to set it up: putting something at risk, making a dangerous proposition, implying the immediate threat of violence, edging toward serious consequences. When you use a Reaction to set it up, you call the other players to action. Something needs to be said or done. What happens next?
Some of the time, you’ll use Reactions to knock it down: bridges burnt, damage done, havoc had. When you use a Reaction to knock it down, you force the players to deal with the consequences of something that just happened. Does the situation continue to escalate? Is the teen social hierarchy restored or disrupted? How do you stop the bleeding? What happens next?
Picture Lily, the spurned cheerleading captain sworn to revenge. Putting a gun in her hand is setting it up.
Putting a bullet in someone’s shoulder is knocking it down.

So how do you know which to do, moment to moment?
The cheeky answer is: you’ll know. The other answer is: if you’ve already set something up, and now people are looking at you expectantly again, knock it down.
